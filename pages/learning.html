Encouragement and a Growth Mindset

Computer Programming is not just a body of knowledge to be learned. It is a skill to be acquired. Like learning to ski, dance, or speak French, it requires more than study. It requires practice. And just like building muscles in your arms and legs, building new neural pathways in your brain takes time.

Many students can be intimidated by computer science. It requires you to learn a lot upfront and your prior knowledge will not help you that much. Many students can struggle at the outset, and that struggle can easily lead to students feeling frustrated and, eventually, concluding that they will never understand this subject. This issue is compounded by the fact that if you make a very small mistake while programming the whole project will not work, and finding that small mistake can be challenging as well. As a teacher you need to be encouraging, always point out that mistakes and bug fixing are issues that ALL programmers deal with. There is nothing wrong with them it is just the nature of the business. You can help encourage students by instilling a growth mindset in them. A growth mindset just means that students believe that they will improve through practice and work. Remind students that no one came out of the womb knowing how to program, that skill was obtained through practice.

Use Visuals

Most concepts in computer science are abstract, they can be hard to understand through just verbal or written descriptions. Visual aids are absolutely critical. Use flowcharts to describe conditionals and loops. Use animated images to show how algorithms manipulate a data set. Use charts and diagrams to describe the architecture of a computer or a network. These things will greatly help your students in understanding some of the more complicated concepts in computer science.

Analyze Existing Code

When teaching someone how to do something it is really useful to show them an example of that technique used well. Show students examples of good code using the concepts you are currently studying and have them analyze. Ask them to identify the techniques used and why they were used. Using this in conjunction with other techniques will help deepen their understanding. You can also use examples of bad code, have them analyze a code segment and find the errors. This will help them anticipate and avoid those same errors when they write their own code.

Collaboration and Pair Programming

Collaboration is a powerful learning tool in any subject. When students work together they are able to learn from each other. The trouble in computer science is that the logistics of having multiple students working on one project can be challenging. In an advanced class you may be able to teach them how to use version control and facilitate group projects in that way. However, in most high school and below classrooms this will not be practical. Some online environment have very nice collaborative tools (CMU CS academy stands out here in my experience). For most situations the solution is pair programming. This is the practice of having two students work at one computer, one observes while the other types. Every few minutes they switch spots. When done well this allows two students to work very closely together on one project. I would advise not using this technique for very long projects, this technique can work well for one, maybe two class periods in a row, but longer than that and students can start to chafe at it, especially if there is a knowledge imbalance or a personality clash.

Using Documentation

Programmers are always looking things up. No programmer actively tries to memorize all of the methods in Java's String class, for example. If you need to know something you don't use all the time you look it up in the documentation. It is important that students also learn how to do this and that doing so is perfectly normal and not "cheating" in any way. When students ask about something that could be found in the documentation, rather than answering them directly have them look it up themselves. If possible, I would also encourage making the documentation available during any exams to further normalize looking things up.

Hands-On

There is no substitute for hands on experience when it comes to programming. No matter what other techniques you use most class time should be taken up by students writing their own code. Make sure to give them projects that force them to use the concept you are learning and then let them go to work. It is through this process that an abstract concept becomes a concrete technique that students can then use whenever they need to.

Accommodations

Universal Accommodations

We recommend a multi-modal approach. In addition to typical lectures and textbooks, we also suggest:

  • Presentation graphics, emphasis on the word graphics: too many PowerPoints are walls of words that don't take advantage of bullet points, images, and animations
  • Web resources, including videos and, if possible, resources in alternate languages - but any sort of web resource lends itself to automatic translation tools.
  • Jigsaw group discussions: student groups are assigned research projects for which they report out.
  • Recorded lectures with closed captioning are great for English Language Learners, those with audio processing issues, and everyone else who may need to review what was said.
  • Tons of lab time. As mentioned above, computer science is only learned by doing.

Students with Learning Disabilities

  • Scaffolding with a skeleton and demo programs that can be used to copy/paste
  • Extra time on assignments and tests
  • Priority seating
  • Tutoring available during office hours/choice time or after school

Students with ADHD

  • Figet spinners
  • Balance balls to sit on
  • Headphones to block sound

Most schools require documentation of a disability in order to offer extra time, but educators often have latitude. Some make it universally available simply by eliminating late penalties.
